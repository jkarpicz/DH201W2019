Art in Public Places Programs

A timeline of the creation of state percent for art programs nationally as well as information regarding other, local percent for art programs across the state of Washington.

There are currently 26 active state percent for art programs in the United States. Additionally, there are 6 programs that either were previously active (North Carolina, Wisconsin, Iowa, Connecticut, Michigan) or have yet to be funded (Massachusetts). Percent for Art programs began to formally emerge in the late 1960s, with the creation of the National Endowment for the Art's Art in Public Places Program in 1967, which over 28 years (until 1995), "provided matching grants for community-initiated public art projects," funding over 700 works of art across the country (Daugherty, North Carolina Arts Council, p. 36). Hawaii's state percent for the art program, Art in Public Places, also began in 1967, the first and oldest program of its kind in the nation. These programs have established a template for appropriating small percentages (0.5 - 1.5%) of construction and renovation budgets to fund the creation, acquisition, installation, and maintainance of public artworks at public sites, including schools, agencies, transit stations, etc. at the city, state, and federal-level. Many cities (ex: Boston, Los Angeles, New York City) as well as higher education institutions (ex: MIT, Brown University, and public institutions in the Texas system) have created their own, local percent for art programs.

The timeline below outlines the emergence of state percent for the art programs from 1967 to the present. These programs emerged most rapidly in the late 1970s and steadily throughout the 1980s, "spurred on by the federally sponsored Art in Public Places Program started in 1967 at the National Endowment for the Arts ... [and] the Art in Architecture Program of the General Services Administration, another federally funded public art program started in 1972, [which] continues to commission artwork for federal buildings across the country" (Daugherty, North Carolina Arts Council, p. 36). A map highlighting the states with with active (green) and inactive (red/orange) programs is included above the timeline for reference [Note: Guam is not visualized on this map, but has an active and robust percent for the art program). As a next step with the project, it would be useful to create a map that shows a timelapse of when programs became (in)active, to identify possible regional trends.

A Culture of Public Art

The State of Washington has multiple public art programs across the state at a city and institutional-level. A few of these programs are included below.

  • Western Washington University created an internal program for designating portions of capital project funds to the creation of public art on-campus years prior to the creation of the Washington Art in Public Places program. Visit their Multimedia Tour of Public Sculpture Collection: A web project with images, art descriptions, and artist information for the indoor and outdoor pieces in Western Washington's sculpture collection. The pieces in the sculpture collection come from a variety of funding sources, not all are funded by the State's Art in Public Places program.
  • City of Seattle Percent for Art: In 1973, a year before Washington created the state Art in Public Places program, the City of Seattle created a 1% for art program that has funded the creation of over 400 pieces of artwork across the city. The city maintains an app with information about these public art installations. Seattle was one of the earliest cities to create such an ordinance, following Philadelphia (1959), Baltimore (1964), and San Francisco (1967).
  • City of Spokane "% for the Art": In 1981, seven years after Washington created the state Art in Public Places program, the City of Spoke passed a city ordinance requiring 1% of select capital construction projects be designated for acquisitions of public artwork. 1% of certain capital construction costs be utilized to purchase artwork for enhancing public buildings and spaces.
