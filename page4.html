The Acquisition Process

The Washington State Art Commission also keeps a roster of artists; institutions can select existing artwork or select an artist based on the type of work they have submitted to the commission. To learn more about the process, read hyperlink.

Many of the institutions in the collection have public art collections that expand beyond those pieces in the State Art Collection (Washington Art in Public Places program). At these institutions, some have publicly available information about their own public art collection processes and policies. Examples of these types of local collections are Evergreen State College's Art Collection Policy (quoted above) and the University of Washington's committee. Both institutions have established selection committees and processes that outline how art is selected for acquistion. Another notable campus collection, featured on this page, is the collection at Western Washington University, which began an internal percent for the arts program based on its capital projects in 1965, almost a decade prior to the creation of the State program. The collection is thoroughly documented in the book, Sculpture in Place: A Campus as Site by Sarah Clark-Langager.

Percent for Art Funding

Half of 1% of funding for new public buildings and restorations that total over $200,000 is allocated for public art.

"With public sculpture, the site is a defining factor"
- Beverly Pepper, sculptor (Clark-Langager, 2002, p. 62)

Site-specific public art is developed in relationship with the place, people, social and environmental history of its location. On college campuses, public art may interpret the learning, natural, geological, social, residential, and/or historical environment.



Most public art pieces acquired for colleges and universities through the Art in Public Places program were created within five years of acquisition. Recently, institutions seem to moving towards acquiring site-specific work.

There has been a trend towards site-specific acquisitions on-campus. The bubble chart below shows for every year that art was acquired, at what point the art was created. Pieces that were created before they were acquired are above the x-axis (x = 2, created two years before acquisition). Pieces that were created after acquisition are below the x-axis (x = -2, created two years after date of acquisition; note that here, creation date is considered to be the date of installation). The size of the bubble captures the number of pieces that fit the coordinates, i.e. there were X number of pieces that were acquisitioned in 1978 and created 1976.

Most of the pieces are clustered around the x-axis, indicating that most public art pieces acquired for colleges and universities through the Art in Public Places program were created within five years before / after the year of acquisition. However, the chart overall appears to trend downwards, suggesting that institutions are moving towards acquiring site-specific pieces. This shift coincides with 

"In the last half of the twentieth century, public art has moved away from discrete  objects placed in public plazas to art that is integrated within a site. In the 1980s and 1990s, there was a move in the public art field to bring artists onto design teams to work with architects to incorporate artwork into the design of a building or plaza, and public art moved toward the goal of creating a sense of place" (North Carolina Arts Council, p. 35). 

"When I was first invited to the campus, I had a blank slate as to what I would be doing. I walked through the whole campus area and came across the plaza where Feats of Strength was eventually placed. I found the plaza really engaging, both the design itself and the way students were using it. My work was in response to what I saw there."
- Tom Otterness, sculptor (Clark-Langager, 2002, p. 88)
